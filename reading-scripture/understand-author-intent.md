# Understand Author Intent

The Bible is God's Word.

It is also a collection of writings given through human authors in specific places, times, and circumstances.

Faithful reading requires us to pay attention to both.

The goal of studying Scripture is not to make a passage say something new.

The goal is to understand what God has communicated through His Word.

## Scripture Was Written to Real People

The books of the Bible were written to actual audiences.

Letters were written to churches facing specific challenges.

Prophets spoke to people in particular moments of history.

Narratives recorded events that happened in real places.

Wisdom literature addressed real questions about life, suffering, and faithfulness.

Understanding the original setting helps us better understand the message.

## Scripture Was Written for Us

Recognizing the original audience does not make Scripture distant.

God's Word continues to speak because its ultimate author is God Himself.

The circumstances may differ.

The principles may require careful application.

But the truth remains.

Scripture was not written directly to every modern reader, but it was written for the instruction, encouragement, correction, and transformation of God's people.

## Ask What the Author Intended

Before asking how a passage applies today, ask:

What was the author communicating?

Why was this written?

Who was the original audience?

What problem was being addressed?

What truth was being emphasized?

What response was being invited?

These questions help us listen before we respond.

## Consider the Context

No verse exists by itself.

Words have meaning within sentences.

Sentences have meaning within paragraphs.

Paragraphs have meaning within books.

Books have meaning within the larger story of Scripture.

A verse removed from its context can easily be misunderstood.

A verse understood within its context becomes clearer and more powerful.

## Respect the Genre

The Bible contains many kinds of writing.

History tells stories of God's work through His people.

Poetry uses imagery and emotion.

Wisdom literature invites reflection.

Prophecy calls people to faithfulness and points toward God's purposes.

Letters teach, encourage, correct, and instruct.

Apocalyptic literature uses vivid imagery to reveal God's sovereignty.

Each genre should be read according to what it is.

## Avoid Making the Bible About Yourself

The Bible speaks to us.

But it is not primarily about us.

Every part of Scripture ultimately points toward God's character, His purposes, and His work of redemption.

The central question is not:

"What does this verse allow me to do?"

or

"What does this verse say about my personal success?"

The deeper question is:

"What does this reveal about God, His kingdom, and His purposes?"

## Application Comes After Understanding

Application is essential.

God's Word is meant to change how we think, believe, and live.

But faithful application begins with faithful understanding.

We do not first decide what a passage means and then look for support.

We first listen carefully.

Then we respond faithfully.

## A Final Encouragement

The Bible is not a book that needs to be reinvented for every generation.

It is a book that needs to be faithfully understood by every generation.

Approach it with humility.

Study it carefully.

Trust that God has spoken.

Then live in response to what He has said.
